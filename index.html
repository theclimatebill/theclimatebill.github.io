The central question

The countries facing the greatest losses are often not the countries producing the most emissions.

Since 1750, industrialised economies built their wealth on fossil fuels. Today the heaviest human and economic costs of a warming climate fall disproportionately on lower income countries that contributed least to the problem. This is documented by the UN, the IPCC, and independent research.

The bill has been agreed. It has not been paid.

After thirty years of argument, the world finally created a fund to compensate countries for climate damage they did not cause. Here is the gap between what was promised and what is needed.

About this project

Why this site exists.

I grew up in Jaisalmer, Rajasthan, one of the hottest places on Earth. There, heat is not a distant policy question. It shapes daily life, work, and survival. Moving to the United Kingdom to study International Relations at the University of Sheffield gave me a different vantage point.

In much of the Global North, climate change is discussed as a future risk to be managed and planned for. In Jaisalmer, in Bangladesh, in northern Kenya, it is a present reality. The distance between how climate change is perceived in wealthier countries and how it is lived in more vulnerable ones is one of the defining gaps of our time. The Climate Bill exists to make that gap visible with reliable data.

My postgraduate research on the EU Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism and power in global climate governance gave me the academic grounding for what experience had already taught me. The rules of the global energy transition are largely written by the countries that industrialised first. The heaviest costs are carried by countries that contributed least.

Every figure on this site comes from official or authoritative sources, including NASA, NOAA, the WMO, the IPCC, the IEA, IRENA, the World Bank, the IMF, the United Nations, and Our World in Data. Each statistic links to its original source so you can verify it yourself.

Methodology and limitations

How this site was built, and what it cannot tell you.

Every data project makes choices. Here are the ones made here, and the places where the numbers should be treated with care.

Source hierarchy

Figures are taken from intergovernmental and national scientific agencies first: NASA, NOAA, the WMO, the IPCC, the IEA, IRENA, the World Bank, the IMF, UNEP and the UNFCCC. Where a peer reviewed study is the best available source, such as the Lancet Countdown on health or the Living Planet Index on wildlife, that is used and named. News organisations are used only for reporting individual events, never for underlying statistics.

What the emissions figures cover

Annual CO2 figures are territorial emissions from fossil fuels and industry, following the Global Carbon Budget convention. They exclude land use change and consumption based accounting. This matters: a country that imports its manufactured goods can appear cleaner than it is. Brazil and Indonesia, whose emissions are heavily land use driven, appear lower here than a full accounting would show.

Three views, three answers

The map can be read as annual, cumulative or per person emissions, and each gives a different answer to the question of who is responsible. None is the single correct view. Annual emissions matter for what happens next. Cumulative emissions matter for who caused the warming already locked in. Per person emissions matter for fairness. The site shows all three deliberately.

The economic loss estimates

Country level loss figures are expressed as an estimated range of annual GDP loss and are drawn from World Bank, IMF and Germanwatch assessments. They are indicative, not precise. Loss estimates vary widely by method, and slow onset damage such as soil salinity or lost labour productivity is systematically undercounted everywhere.

Known limitations

Renewable share figures refer to electricity generation, not total energy, which includes transport, heating and industry. A country can have a clean grid and still run largely on fossil fuels. Country profiles cover the largest emitters and most affected nations rather than all 195 states. The verdict labels are editorial judgements informed by Climate Action Tracker, not official assessments.

Corrections

If you find an error, a broken source link, or a figure that has since been superseded, please write to theclimatebill@gmail.com. Corrections are made promptly and this page notes when the data was last reviewed. The site is a work in progress and is offered in that spirit.
